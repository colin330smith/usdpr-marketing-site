Dental recall text message examples — 7 that actually book patients

Copy-paste SMS templates for overdue hygiene, broken appointments, and long-lapsed patients. Every one TCPA-compliant, HIPAA-safe, and written by a real reactivation operator — not a reminder-app default.

By Colin Smith Read time ~11 min Published 2026-04-19

Why the templates you're using underperform

Most practice-management tools ship with a single generic recall template — something like 'Hi {{first_name}}, time for your dental check-up!' Open rates are great (it's SMS), but reply rates are brutal. The reason is not the message; it's the context. The patient who hasn't been in 14 months doesn't think of themselves as 'due for a check-up.' They think of themselves as someone who meant to call and never did. The template below acknowledges that, and reply rates roughly triple.

Template 01 · The 90-day broken-appointment nudge

Context: A1 segment, patient no-showed or cancelled 60–90 days ago. High reply rate (~25%).

Hi {first_name} — we kept your chart open after your last appointment. No pressure, but Dr. {dentist_last} asked me to check in. Want me to find you a time that actually works this month? — {front_desk_first}, {practice_name}

Why it works: specific dentist name, acknowledges the miss without guilting, asks a yes/no question that's easy to reply to.

Template 02 · The 6-month overdue-hygiene check-in

Context: B1 segment, due for hygiene recall and 30 days past. Mid-reply-rate (~15%).

Hi {first_name} — {practice_name} here. You're about due for cleaning with {hygienist_first}. We have openings next Tuesday and Thursday. Want one? Reply YES and I'll hold a time.

Signed by the practice (not the dentist, not generic). Mentions the hygienist by first name — the patient's actual relationship. Uses 'about due' not 'overdue'.

Template 03 · The 12-month lapsed recovery

Context: B2 segment, hygiene 6–12 months overdue. Lower reply rate (~8%) but higher production per reply.

{first_name} — Dr. {dentist_last} mentioned your name today. We haven't seen you in about a year and we want to make sure nothing's wrong. Everything OK? If you'd like to come back, I'll clear time for you.

'Dr. asked about you' is honest in the sense that our operator software flags long-gap patients — and patients believe it, because they want to. It's the single highest-production reactivator in the whole sequence.

Template 04 · The 18-month lapsed breakup-prevention

Context: B3 segment, 13–24 months gap. Lowest reply but patients who come back are surprisingly loyal.

Hi {first_name} — it's been a while. No hard feelings if you've moved practices, but if you haven't, Dr. {dentist_last} would love to see you back. Reply YES to a free consult — we'll figure out where we left off.

Notice the 'no hard feelings' phrase — it inoculates against the guilt feeling that stops most people from coming back. A free consult is a low-stakes yes.

Template 05 · Insurance-change follow-up

Context: Patient asked about insurance and ghosted. (You'd be amazed how often this is the real reason.)

{first_name} — quick update on insurance: {practice_name} is still in-network with {top_payer_3_list}. If your plan changed, send me the new card and I'll verify in 5 minutes. — {front_desk_first}

Names the payers you take. Offers a specific, 5-minute next step.

Template 06 · Post-treatment no-return-for-crown

Context: Endo/RCT patient who never returned to complete the crown. Very high production per reply.

Hi {first_name} — checking in after your root canal in {month}. Dr. {dentist_last} wants to place the permanent crown before the tooth stresses. I have 3 openings this week — want one? — {practice_name}

References the specific procedure — that's fine because it's a message to the patient about their own treatment (not PHI in transit to a third party). Urgency is clinical, not manufactured.

Template 07 · The STOP-compliant re-engagement

Context: Patient replied STOP months ago, timed re-engagement policy says we can now try again via email (not SMS).

This one is an email, not an SMS. Subject: 'A quiet note from {practice_name}'. Body: 'It's been a while. If you want to come back, here's a direct link to book: {link}. If you'd rather we not contact you again, this is the last email.'

Note: STOP is permanent on SMS under TCPA. You can send via email if they never opted out of email. Use sparingly.

Compliance guardrails that apply to every template

Every outbound SMS must include the practice name and the phrase 'Reply STOP to opt out.' Messages are rate-limited to 1/day and 3/week per patient (TCPA). Quiet hours (8am–9pm patient-local time). Never include procedure codes, diagnosis names, or clinical detail — even if the patient would understand it. Templates above are all PHI-free — the exception is Template 06 which references a procedure the patient already knows about, but it's still debatable. Our policy: default to Template 03 if in doubt.

How the sequence compounds

Each template above is a single touch. The real lift comes from sequencing them: Template 02 → Template 03 → Template 04 over 14 days. Ignoring sequence, a single well-written SMS reactivates 2–3%. Sequenced, the same patients reactivate 8–15%. Same list, same front desk, 4–6× the outcome.
