How to reactivate lapsed dental patients — the 5-touch method that actually works

The field-tested sequence that takes your 1–3% single-send reactivation rate up to 8–15%. Written by the operator of a orthodontic patient recovery SaaS — no fluff, no fillers, real cadence and real copy.

By Colin Smith Read time ~14 min Published 2026-04-19

The math of the leak (so you know what we're chasing)

A 2,000-patient practice loses roughly 180 active patients per year at a 9% lapse rate — ADA/Levin Group benchmark. At a conservative 1.5-visit × $285-per-visit multiplier, that's ~$77,000 in unrealized production per year. Most practices try to plug this with a quarterly SMS blast or a January postcard mailer. Those get 1–3% reactivation. The five-touch sequence below consistently hits 8–15%. Run your exact numbers.

The four segments that matter

Before any outreach goes out, segment the list. Four windows explain 80% of the reactivation variance:

  • A1 — recent broken appointment (0–60 days). Warmest segment. Don't need a marketing voice.
  • A2 — older broken appointment (60–180 days). Starts to need an acknowledgment.
  • B1 — overdue hygiene (0–90 days past recall). Routine reminder-adjacent.
  • B2 — overdue hygiene (90–365 days past recall). Needs a reset, not a reminder.
  • B3 — overdue hygiene (365+ days). Lowest reply rate but highest production if recovered.

Most PMS exports have the data fields to segment on — last appointment date, last hygiene visit, appointment status. If yours doesn't, CSV-massage it.

Touch 01 · Email · Day 0

Always email first. Zero carrier approval wait (you can literally send today), highest deliverability, longest content window. Make it sound like the practice manager wrote it from their own inbox. No marketing-agency signature, no stock photo of a dentist, no 'special offer!' framing. One short paragraph, one specific next action.

Touch 02 · SMS · Day 3

SMS to the same segment, referencing the email gently. 'Hi {first_name} — sent you a note on Monday about getting you back on the schedule, wasn't sure it landed. Want me to find you a time?' — signs with the front-desk person's first name. Under 160 characters. Rate-limited to 1/day, 3/week per patient under TCPA.

Touch 03 · Email · Day 7

Second email, shifted subject line. If the day-0 email was 'A note from {practice_name}', day 7 should be 'Still good for a cleaning?' — the shift matters. Open rate on the second subject line is usually 10–15 percentage points higher because it reads differently in the preview pane. Content: offer two specific times. Not 'pick a time' — two actual times.

Touch 04 · Phone · Day 10

A live call from the practice. The front desk has 5 replies to work with at this point (from touches 1-3) and a list of patients who haven't replied. Call the non-repliers in batches of 10. Script: 'Hi, this is {first_name} from {practice_name}. Dr. {dentist_last} asked me to check in — we haven't seen you in a while. Do you have 30 seconds?' No voicemail, no 30-minute phone pitch. Just a reset moment.

Touch 05 · SMS · Day 14

Final SMS, shorter, almost apologetic. 'Hi {first_name} — last check-in from us this month. If you want back on the schedule, reply YES. If not, no hard feelings.' This line is specifically what moves B3-segment patients who've been too awkward to respond. The explicit 'no hard feelings' permission to say no is — paradoxically — what makes them say yes.

Why sequencing > any single message

A single send reactivates 1–3% regardless of which channel you pick. Five sends together — same patients — reactivate 8–15%. The multiplier isn't additive; it's the compounding effect of (a) channel-switching (email people ignore SMS they see; SMS people ignore email they receive), (b) a call that demonstrates real attention, and (c) the explicit permission-to-opt-out on touch 5 removing social friction. Leave any one touch out and the rate collapses back toward single-send.

What to not do

Don't send the same blast four times. Don't include procedure codes or treatment detail in SMS (TCPA + HIPAA will hurt you). Don't run this sequence without a BAA signed with whatever vendor is handling the outbound. Don't discount — the moment you introduce a coupon, you attract the wrong kind of reactivation. Don't skip the phone call (touch 4) — it's the step dentists most want to skip, and it's the one that produces the most bookings by dollar value.
