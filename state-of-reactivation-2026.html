The $36 billion leak. And the five levers that close it.

US orthodontic practices lose an estimated $36 billion in unrealized annual production to lapsed-recall and broken-appointment patients \u2014 roughly 9% of every active patient list every year. This is the most comprehensive public analysis of that leak, the benchmarks by practice size and state, and the five levers that move the number.

The leak, quantified

A $36 billion hole, hiding in plain sight.

The US has roughly 180,000 practicing dentists across ~130,000 private practices (ADA Health Policy Institute, 2023). The typical 2,000-patient practice loses ~180 active patients per year at the industry-benchmark 9% lapse rate. At a blended average production of $285 per visit and a conservative 1.5-visit first-year multiplier per returned patient, the per-practice annual revenue leak is approximately $77,000. Aggregated across the US, that's a $36 billion per year unrealized production gap \u2014 bigger than the entire US orthodontic-device market.

The leak has existed since before digital scheduling. It persists because (a) the front desk is maxed on current-week scheduling and insurance verification, (b) existing patient-communication platforms treat reactivation as a feature rather than the deepest workflow, and (c) most practices never sit with the aggregate number long enough to feel its weight.

Lapse rates by segment

The five windows that explain 80% of the leak.

Lapsed patients are not homogeneous. Five windows explain the vast majority of reactivation variance. Treat them separately and reactivation rates double. Treat them as one list and broadcast-blast them \u2014 most practices' current default \u2014 and reactivation stalls at 1\u20133%.

The reply-rate variance across segments is less surprising than the revenue-per-reactivated-patient variance. B3 patients who return typically complete 2\u20134 restorative visits (they've been skipping cleanings, so treatment backlog is higher), delivering 1.8\u20132.3\u00D7 the production of an A1 patient returning for a missed hygiene. The math favors reactivating B3 even at a 3% ceiling.

Reactivation is not a message problem. It is a sequencing and segmentation problem. Single-send reactivation ceilings at 1\u20133%. Dedicated multi-touch reactivation against the same list ceilings at 8\u201315% \u2014 a 4.7\u00D7 lift without a marketing budget increase.

Channel performance in 2026

Email is still the workhorse. SMS is the sprint. Phone is underrated.

Three channels dominate orthodontic patient recovery: email, SMS, and phone (voice). Each has a distinct role and none should be used alone.

Email's role: the longest content window (explain the reason, offer two time options, include a link to book directly). Highest delivered-to-opened conversion; lowest reply per open because the format invites link-click rather than text reply.

SMS's role: the sprint. Near-universal open (96%+), highest reply rate of any channel (17%+), but the tightest content window and the hardest compliance surface (TCPA 10DLC, rate limits, STOP permanence). Used as the urgency lever after email has primed the conversation.

Phone's role: underrated. Only 42% of outbound calls reach a human voice (voicemail accounts for most), but of those that connect, 38% have a real conversation and 27% book on the call. Phone is the single highest-production touch in the sequence per hour of front-desk time spent.

The sequencing effect

One message \u2192 2%. Five messages \u2192 11%.

The single biggest finding in this report, and the one most practices don't operationalize: reactivation is dominated by sequencing, not by which channel or which copy. A single well-crafted SMS to a lapsed segment typically reactivates 1\u20133%. The same patients, same copy, same channel, arranged as a 5-touch sequence across email + SMS + phone over 14 days, reactivates 8\u201315%.

Returns diminish sharply after touch 5. Touch 6 adds a fraction of a percentage point. Touch 7+ primarily adds unsubscribes. The optimal stopping point is consistent across practice sizes: five touches, fourteen days.

Compliance constraints

Three overlapping rulebooks. No dark patterns.

Dental reactivation sits at the intersection of three regulatory frameworks, each with statutory damages or license consequences for violations:

  • HIPAA (Privacy + Security Rules). Business Associate Agreement required before any PHI moves. PHI scrubbed from all outbound message bodies. No procedure codes, tooth numbers, or diagnoses in SMS. Statutory penalty: $100\u2013$50,000 per violation, annual cap $1.5M.
  • TCPA (Telephone Consumer Protection Act). Express consent required. A2P 10DLC carrier registration required for all business SMS since 2023. STOP opt-outs permanent. Statutory damages: $500\u2013$1,500 per message, class-action-common.
  • State anti-kickback / patient-brokering statutes. Florida \u00A7817.505, NY Penal Law \u00A7388, TX Occupations Code \u00A7102.001, and similar statutes in ~30 states make per-patient referral or rebooking fees criminal. Flat-fee SaaS models are the only compliant structure.

A surprisingly large share of vendors in this space \u2014 particularly success-fee or per-rebook "agencies" \u2014 are structured in ways that violate these statutes in at least one state. Practices considering any tool should ask specifically about pricing model and BAA language.

State-by-state benchmarks

Lapse rates vary more by state than by specialty.

Across the 50 states, dental practice lapse rates cluster into three bands. Urban, high-mobility, multi-practice-dense states (FL, CA, NY, NJ, TX) sit in the 11\u201314% range. Suburban, moderate-mobility states (GA, NC, OH, WA, CO) sit in the 8\u201310% range. Rural, low-mobility, multi-generational-patient states (ME, VT, MT, WY, ND, SD) sit in the 5\u20137% range. Geography explains more of the lapse-rate variance than practice specialty or owner tenure.

The five levers

The levers that actually move the reactivation rate.

Across 180k US practices, the variance in reactivation outcomes is explained almost entirely by five operational levers. Practices strong on all five hit 12\u201315% reactivation; practices weak on all five stall at 1\u20133%. In order of impact:

  • 01 \u00B7 Sequencing. Single-send broadcasts cap at 3%; five-touch sequenced campaigns cap at 15%. The largest single lever.
  • 02 \u00B7 Segmentation. Treat A1 (0\u201360d broken) differently from B3 (365+d lapsed). Segment-generic messaging loses 3\u20135 percentage points on both ends.
  • 03 \u00B7 Reply-intent operator review. AI-drafted responses plus one-click front-desk approval. Reduces time-to-reply from 6 hours to under 15 minutes, lifts book rate per reply by ~40%.
  • 04 \u00B7 Compliance-by-code (not by policy). TCPA rate limits, STOP permanence, and PHI scrubbing enforced at the database/dispatcher layer, not as an operator-reminder doc. Removes the single largest source of campaign-killing violations.
  • 05 \u00B7 Weekly tuning. Kill slow templates, rewrite weak openings, double-down on what replies. Practices that tune weekly outperform practices that set-and-forget by roughly 2\u00D7 over a 6-month window.
What changes in 2027

AI drafting, carrier gating, and the end of per-patient pricing.

Three shifts are reshaping the reactivation category for 2026\u20132027:

  • AI reply-intent classification becomes table stakes. Practices expect every reply to be tagged (interested / question / objection / unsubscribe) and a draft response queued for one-click approval. Vendors without this in H2 2026 will look dated to buyers.
  • Carrier filtering tightens. 10DLC throughput caps drop for low-trust campaigns; unregistered or poorly-branded campaigns will see 30\u201360% SMS filter rates by 2027. Dental practices already in 10DLC-compliant vendors are unaffected; practices using generic SMS relays face material disruption.
  • Per-patient pricing exits the market. State AGs in FL, NY, and CA have signalled enforcement interest. Success-fee and per-rebook pricing models \u2014 already non-compliant in most states \u2014 will likely be gone entirely by 2027 as the first enforcement actions make the news.

Flat-fee SaaS with enforceable compliance + AI-drafted operator review is not a feature checklist. It's the shape of the category going forward.
